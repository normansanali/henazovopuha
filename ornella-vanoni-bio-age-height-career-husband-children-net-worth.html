Biography of Ornella Vanoni

Ornella Vanoni, an esteemed Italian singer born on September 22, 1934, has left an indelible mark on the music industry with a career spanning nearly seven decades. Recognized with the Order of Merit of the Italian Republic (OMRI), Vanoni embarked on her artistic journey in 1960 as a theater actress, captivating audiences with her performances in Bertolt Brecht works under the direction of Giorgio Strehler at Milan’s Piccolo Teatro.

Simultaneously nurturing a musical career, Vanoni explored folklore and popular songs, earning the moniker “cantante della mala” for her renditions of Milanese songs depicting the criminal underworld. Her breakthrough came in 1963 with the hits “Senza Fine” and “Che cosa c’è,” both penned by Gino Paoli. The following year, she secured victory at the Festival of Neapolitan Song with “Tu si na cosa grande.”

Vanoni’s participation in several San Remo Festivals showcased her versatility, featuring memorable performances like “Abbracciami forte” (1965) and “La musica è finita” (1967). Notably, her song “Casa Bianca” (1968) sparked a copyright dispute, highlighting her enduring impact on Italian music.

The late 1960s saw Vanoni recording notable tracks such as “Una ragione di più” and “L’appuntamento,” a cover of the Brazilian song “Sentado à Beira do Caminho.” In 1972, she lent her voice to “Quei giorni insieme a te,” the theme for Lucio Fulci’s film “Don’t Torture a Duckling.”

Collaborating with Vinicius de Moraes and Toquinho in 1976 on “La voglia, la pazzia, l’incoscienza e l’allegria,” Vanoni continued to evolve her sound. The 1980s witnessed the release of albums like “Ricetta di donna,” “Uomini,” and “Ti lascio una canzone.”

Beyond music, Vanoni ventured into stage and TV shows, earning further acclaim for her diverse talents. In 1977, she made headlines by posing nude for the Italian edition of Playboy, requesting a statuette by her friend, the artist Arnaldo Pomodoro, as payment.

Her song “L’Appuntamento” gained international recognition when featured in Steven Soderbergh’s “Ocean’s Twelve” in 2004, sparking a renewed global interest in her music. Subsequently, the soundtrack of the Danish film “Toscana” (2022, Netflix) continued to showcase the timeless appeal of her work.

With an extensive discography, including albums like “Un gioco senza età” (1972), “Sogni proibiti” (2002), and recent releases such as “Unica” (2021), Ornella Vanoni stands as an enduring icon in Italian music, captivating generations with her evocative voice and multifaceted artistic contributions. Her impact extends beyond the realm of music, marking her as a cultural luminary with an enduring legacy.
